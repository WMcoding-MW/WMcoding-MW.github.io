More on Symmetric groups and cycles April 14th, 2022 6:08 PM

Def: Let $k$, ... ,$k_n \in$ {1,...,m} be distinct, n $\leq$m, Then the permutation of $S_m$ which sends $k_1 |\to k_2$, ... , $k_{n-1} |\to k_n$ and $k_n |\to k_1$ and fixes all other numbers is denoted ($k_1k_2...k_n$) and called an r-cycle (cycle of length r).

Remark: The only 1-cycle is the identity permutation on $S_n$, (1)=(2)=...=(m).

Def: Cycles of length $\geq$ 2 $\in S_m$ are disjoint if they involve disjoint sets of numbers. Ex: (12),(34) are disjoint in $S_n$ but (12),(31) are not.

Remark: Actually every permutation in $S_n \neq Id_{S_n}$ can be written as a product of disjoint cycles of length $\geq$ 2. Moreover, this factorization is unique up to the order of the factors. Ex in $S_5$: (145) o (23) = (23) o (145) => we can't hope for unicity of the decomponents.

Sketch of the proof:let $\sigma \in S_m$. Take a number $k \in$ {1,...,m} and consider $k, \sigma(k), \sigma (\sigma(k)),...$ and $k_1, k_2, k_3, ...$ It must come back to the previous value since the maximal value of k is m. Thus this results in a cycle.
