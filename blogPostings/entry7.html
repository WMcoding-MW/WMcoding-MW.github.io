Order of a group/element and inverses April 15th, 2022 10:58 AM

Def: For a group (G,*) (we often just denote this as G, since a binary operation is implied) the number of elements (cardinal) of G is denoted as |G| and called the order of the group G. (can be infinite)

Ex: ($\mathbb{Z}$,+) has infinite order, ($S_m$, o) has an order of m!.

  • Some properties of groups

    • Let G be a group

    The identity element, $e_G$ is unique.

    proof: for the sake of contradiction, let e and i be two unique identity elements of G. 1) e * a = a * e = a, 2) i * a = a * i = a, $\forall a \in G$. but i = e * i = e, thus they must be the same which contradicts our assumption.

    The inverse of an element is unique. Lemme: (Cancellation lemma) 1)a*b = a*c and 2)b*a = c*a both imply that b=c $\forall a,b,c \in G.$

    Proof: 1) multiply by $a^{-1}$ on the left. $a^{-1}$ * (a * b) = $a^{-1}$ * (a * c) => $(a^{-1}$ * a) * b = ($a^{-1}$ * a) * c => e*b=e*c, thus b=c. similar proof follows for 2, just do it on the right.

    Remark: a*b=c*a DOES NOT IMPLY b=c.

    Proof of uniquness of inverses: let b,c be inverses of a, then b*a = e = c*a. with the cancellation lemma we can see b = c.

    Powers of an element of a group G. Let m$\in \mathbb{Z}$, $g \in G$. g^m := {e if m = 0, g * g * ... * g if m > 0, $g^{-1} * g^{-1} * ... * g^{-1}$ if m < 0}

    Ex: G = $\frac{\mathbb{Z}}{2\mathbb{Z}}, g=[1]_2$

    Then $g^{-1} = [-1]_2 = [1]_2, g^0 = e = [0]_2, g^1 = [1]_2, g^2 = [1]_2 + [1]_2 = [2]_2 = [0]_2, g^3 = [3]_2 = [1]_2 and so on.

    Remark: Because of Associativity in groups, it doesn't matter where one puts the paranthesis in the product.

    Note: However some don't necesarily have $(g*h)^m \neq g^m * h^m.

    Ex: m = 2 then $(g*h)^2 = g * h * g * h = g^2 * h^2 = g * g * h * h <=> h*g = g*h.$

    Inverse of a product

    1) $(a*b)^{-1}$ = $b^{-1} * a^{-1}$

    2) $(a_1 * a_2 * ... * a_m)^{-1} = a_m^{-1} * ... * a_2^{-1} * a_1^{-1}

    Proof: $(a*b) * (b^{-1} * a^{-1})$ = e => $a*(b * b^{-1}) * a^{-1}$ = e => $a * e * a^{-1}$ = e => e=e, thus $(a*b)^{-1}$ = $b^{-1} * a^{-1}$

    remark: In ($\mathbb{Z},+), g^2$ when g = 3; $g^2$=g*g=3+3=6 so $3^2$ = 6, Since the binary operation is addition.
